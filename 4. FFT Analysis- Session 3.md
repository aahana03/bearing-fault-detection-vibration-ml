# FFT Analysis of Session 3

During the comparative analysis of feature distributions across multiple experimental sessions, **Session 3 exhibited anomalous behavior**. In particular, spectral entropy and kurtosis did not follow the expected trends observed in other sessions. Spectral centroid values were spread out over the same region.

These inconsistencies suggested that the fault characteristics were potentially influenced by **external factors such as structural vibrations, sensor mounting conditions, or environmental noise**. This could imply that a dominant signal due to one of these external factors might be masking the actual signal.

To investigate the underlying cause of these deviations, a **frequency-domain analysis using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)** was performed to enable the identification of:

- Additional frequency components  
- Broadband noise  
- Structural resonance effects  

The objective of this analysis was to determine whether the observed feature inconsistencies could be attributed to changes in the spectral characteristics of the vibration signal.

### Feature Behavior in Session 3

In Session 3, several features exhibited deviations from expected trends. Impulsiveness-based features such as kurtosis and crest factor, along with spectral entropy, showed higher values for the healthy condition compared to faulty cases. In contrast, energy-based features such as RMS and standard deviation maintained the expected trend, with higher values for inner and outer race faults.

This behavior can be explained by the dominance of structural vibrations and environmental noise in this session. The FFT analysis revealed a strong low-frequency component in faulty signals, leading to a concentration of spectral energy and consequently lower entropy. Meanwhile, the healthy signal exhibited a broader frequency distribution, resulting in higher entropy.

Additionally, impulsive noise or minor disturbances in the healthy signal likely contributed to elevated kurtosis and crest factor values, as these features are highly sensitive to outliers. The spectral centroid remained similar across all fault types, indicating that it was not sufficiently sensitive to the observed variations.

These observations highlight that feature effectiveness is highly dependent on signal characteristics, and that certain features may become unreliable under conditions where structural or environmental effects dominate over fault-induced signatures.
