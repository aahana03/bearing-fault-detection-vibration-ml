# Experimental Vibration Analysis

## Overview
This section presents the development and evaluation of a machine learning-based bearing fault detection system using vibration data collected from a custom experimental setup.

Unlike benchmark datasets, this data captures **real-world variability across multiple sessions**.

Type of fault introduced: Distributed Wear

---

## Experimental Setup

- Bearing mounted inside a housing placed on a rigid plate  
- Accelerometer mounted on the housing  
- Constant RPM operating conditions   
- Data collected across multiple sessions  
- ESP32 and MPU6050 used to acquire vibration data

---

## Objective
- To evaluate the performance of various ML models on **real experimental vibration data**.
- To study the effect of session variability and structural resonance on the quality of vibration signals.

---

## Methodology

### Feature Extraction
Raw acceleration data was recorded using accelerometer and micrcontroller and the following features were extracted along x, y and z- axes:

- RMS  
- Standard Deviation  
- Kurtosis  
- Crest Factor  
- Spectral Centroid  
- Spectral Entropy  

### Feature Engineering
- Resultant features computed from multi-axis data to ensure orientation independence

### Kurtosis Variation Across Sessions
![Kurtosis Plot](https://github.com/aahana03/bearing-fault-detection-vibration-ml/blob/592ee7b2bd57fbd21e25ad571488d7e10ad4a197/Kurtosis%20variation%20across%20sessions.png)

Kurtosis values did not consistently distinguish between fault types across sessions. This can be attributed to the introduction of distributed wear, which produces continuous surface roughness rather than localized impulsive impacts.

Unlike localized defects such as cracks or spalls that generate sharp transient responses, distributed wear leads to more uniform vibration characteristics, resulting in lower and less distinctive kurtosis values. Additionally, kurtosis is highly sensitive to outliers, which explains the presence of occasional extreme values even in healthy signals, likely caused by environmental disturbances or measurement noise.

These observations indicate that kurtosis is less effective for detecting distributed fault conditions and should be used in conjunction with other features for reliable classification.

---

### Spectral Entropy Variation
![Entropy Plot](https://github.com/aahana03/bearing-fault-detection-vibration-ml/blob/6dfb2415b38207816e73269709e7435995e2ed85/Spectral%20Entropy%20Variation%20across%20sessions.png)

While spectral entropy generally increases for faulty conditions due to higher signal complexity, inconsistencies were observed across sessions. Especially in session 3, healthy signals exhibited higher entropy values, likely due to structural vibrations, background noise, or changes in mounting conditions.

This indicates that while spectral entropy is less sensitive to amplitude variations, it remains sensitive to environmental and structural factors that influence the distribution of spectral energy. Therefore, it should be used in combination with other features for robust fault classification.

### Heatmap
![Heatmap Plot](https://github.com/aahana03/bearing-fault-detection-vibration-ml/blob/27ae1a9afd5abacfad1e86605f06524643848153/Heatmap.png)


A correlation analysis was performed to examine the relationships between extracted vibration features and the target variable (fault type), as well as inter-feature dependencies.

Key observations:

- A strong positive correlation is observed between **resultant RMS** and **resultant standard deviation** (~0.76), indicating that both features primarily capture the overall energy content of the vibration signal. This suggests potential redundancy within energy-based descriptors.

- **Kurtosis** and **crest factor** show moderate correlation (~0.58), consistent with their shared sensitivity to impulsive events generated by localized defects such as spalls or cracks.

- Among all features, **spectral entropy** exhibits the highest correlation with the target variable (~0.50).

- **Spectral centroid** demonstrates relatively low correlation with other features, suggesting that it contributes complementary frequency-domain information and enhances feature diversity.

- The majority of feature pairs exhibit low to moderate correlation, indicating that the feature set retains a balance between redundancy and independence, which is beneficial for machine learning model performance.

Overall, the correlation structure highlights that the selected features collectively capture distinct physical characteristics of the vibration signal, including energy distribution, impulsiveness, and spectral content. This diversity in representation contributes to improved fault classification performance, particularly under varying experimental conditions.

Binary classification was performed:
- Healthy  
- Faulty

### Model Performance on Unseen Session Data
![confusion Matrix](https://github.com/aahana03/bearing-fault-detection-vibration-ml/blob/b1d701e06b70bbec78316675141036195717ef8c/KNN%20Confusion%20Matrix.png)

Three models were selected for evaluation on unseen session data:

- Tuned SVC  
- KNN  
- Tuned KNN  

| Model        | Accuracy |
|--------------|----------|
| Tuned SVC    | ~70%     |
| KNN          | 95%      |
| Tuned KNN    | 95.5%    |

Despite achieving ~70% accuracy, the tuned SVC model failed to generalize effectively. It predominantly classified all samples as faulty. Due to class imbalance (faulty samples being approximately three times the healthy samples), this resulted in deceptively moderate accuracy while completely misclassifying the minority (healthy) class.

In contrast, both KNN and tuned KNN demonstrated strong generalization performance, correctly identifying both healthy and faulty samples. The tuned KNN model achieved a marginal improvement (~0.5%) over KNN by correctly classifying one additional healthy instance.

### Key Insight

- This highlights the limitation of accuracy as a sole evaluation metric in imbalanced datasets. While SVC achieved moderate accuracy, its predictions lacked class discrimination. On the other hand, KNN-based models maintained balanced performance across classes, making them more reliable for fault detection in real-world scenarios.

- The results indicate that instance-based models such as KNN are better suited for handling distribution variability and class imbalance in experimental vibration data, whereas margin-based models like SVC may suffer from biased decision boundaries under such conditions.

---

## Challenges & Limitations

While the selected features generally demonstrated effective fault discrimination, inconsistencies were observed across different experimental sessions.

In particular, spectral entropy, which typically showed higher values for faulty conditions, exhibited deviations in certain sessions. In one session, cage fault entropy values were lower than expected, while in another, healthy signals exhibited the highest entropy. These observations indicate that spectral entropy, although robust to amplitude variations, is sensitive to changes in signal complexity arising from external factors.

Such variations can be attributed to:
- Structural vibrations of the mounting plate  
- Changes in sensor mounting conditions  
- Background noise and environmental disturbances  

These factors can introduce additional frequency components, leading to an increased spread of spectral energy and consequently higher entropy values, even in the absence of faults.

## Conclusion

These findings highlight that:
- No single feature is sufficient for robust fault detection under real-world conditions  
- Features capturing global signal complexity (e.g., spectral entropy) can be influenced by environmental factors  
- Combining multiple features is essential to distinguish fault-induced characteristics from structural or noise-induced variations  

To further investigate these anomalies, **FFT-based analysis** was performed on session 3.
